GUNUNG KINABALU

FEEL THE WIND

Mount Kinabalu is not only the iconic landmark of Sabah, but also a symbol of culture, history, and unmatched natural beauty. As one of the tallest mountains in Southeast Asia, it is more than just a tourist destination — it is a place rich with legends and local beliefs. In this article, we will explore the myths, historical background, and cultural significance of this majestic mountain in Sabah.

The name “Kinabalu” is believed to come from the Dusun language — combining “Aki” meaning ancestor or spirit, and “Nabalu” meaning mountain. Therefore, “Akinabalu” can be interpreted as the Mountain of the Spirits of the Ancestors. This reflects the local belief that Mount Kinabalu is a resting place for the souls of the departed.

One of the most well-known legends tells the story of a Chinese princess who waited faithfully for her lover, a local warrior, to return from a sea voyage. Unfortunately, he never came back. The princess waited atop the mountain until her death, and her spirit is said to still dwell on the mountain. This romantic and tragic tale adds a mystical element to Mount Kinabalu. For the Kadazan-Dusun people, Mount Kinabalu is considered sacred. Every year, before major climbing expeditions, a ritual known as monolob is performed by a bobolian (a traditional priest or shaman). This ceremony involves the sacrifice of a chicken and prayers in an ancient dialect, seeking permission and protection from the mountain spirits.

Mount Kinabalu was first documented in modern history by Hugh Low, a British administrator who climbed the mountain in 1851. The summit route he took is now known as “Low’s Peak” in his honor. Since then, the mountain has attracted climbers, adventurers, and researchers from around the world. Mount Kinabalu is now part of Kinabalu National Park, which was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2000. It is home to more than 5,000 species of plants, including the world’s largest flower — the Rafflesia. The mountain also plays a major role in Sabah’s economy through eco-tourism and biological research. Mount Kinabalu is more than just a towering peak — it is a symbol of Sabah’s rich cultural heritage and spiritual beliefs. From mythical tales of undying love to sacred rituals passed down through generations, the mountain holds countless stories that connect people, nature, and the unseen world. It remains a place of adventure, respect, and deep reflection.

Gunung Kinabalu is the highest mountain in Malaysia and one of the tallest in Southeast Asia, standing proudly at 4,095 meters (13,435 feet) above sea level. Located in the Malaysian state of Sabah on the island of Borneo, the mountain is the crown jewel of Kinabalu Park, which was designated Malaysia’s first UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2000. Its breathtaking presence dominates the landscape and symbolizes the natural beauty of Sabah.
